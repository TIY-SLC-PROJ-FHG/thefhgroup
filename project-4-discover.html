Goal 1: Get organized for research. Vault

Taking the time to get well-organized now will save you time later, and it will help you research your ancestor lines efficiently.

Choices

A Create a research binder to organize your materials.

  1. Get a binder and add page dividers in it, by geographic place of research or family surname.
  2. Determine your own sub-categories within the dividers, such as maps, how-to's, information contacts and web sites, where to order records, printed forms, brief blog posts and articles, etc.

B Create and use "to-do" lists to keep on track with your research.

This helps you remember where to begin and how to stay on target with your goals.

  1. Study these videos for tips on writing down research learnings and keeping effective to-do lists. |
  2. Decide on small, focused tasks for your to-do list. Make sure your tasks support the goal(s) you are working on.
  3. Use an electronic to-do list (Outlook, iPhone, etc.) or a paper-based list for your to-do list.
  4. In FamilySearch or Ancestry, use the Comments field on a person's record to store to-do items. Delete the items when you are finished with them.

C Keep your records organized as you research.

  1. Use tips from these videos to help you stay organized with your research. |
  2. Read these articles for tips on organizing your research and your findings. |
  3. Read this article for tips on taking effective notes as you research.
  4. Use research logs to track your progress. |
  5. Store lists of records you are working on in the FamilySearch Source Box or the Ancestry Shoebox.

D Keep track of your correspondence with others so you don't duplicate efforts.

  1. Read this article, including the embedded links, for ideas on tracking correspondence.
  2. Make use of e-mail or text-message folders for electronic correspondence.

E Use pre-printed charts to help you organize your research efforts.

  1. Use an Ancestral Chart for sketching ancestor possibilities.
  2. Use a Research Calendar to keep track of resources you have explored and other notes.
  3. Use a Research Extract to summarize data you have researched, when digital copies are not possible or practical.
  4. Use a Correspondence Record to track correspondence with others.
  5. Use a Family Group Sheet to record information on members of a family you are researching.
  6. Use Source Summary to summarize sources for a given family.

Goal 2: Identify a line to do research on, to extend your pedigree.

Choosing one line at a time to pursue is a more efficient way to do research. In general, try to gather as much information about a given family unit as possible, before tracing back additional generations. Here is a warmup video to get you started with research; watch 7:10 to 8:15.

Choices

A In Family Tree, find a family group sheet with some missing information for ancestors you want to find.

  1. If possible, look for someone who was born after 1700, with a recorded spouse.
  2. Make note of missing names, dates and locations in the records of recent ancestors.
  3. Here are some tips for choosing a line to research.
  4. Read this About.com article for tips on finding missing parents in your line.
  5. Plan to search for all your ancestors, not just the direct male or female lines.

BForm questions for each selected ancestor that will help drive your research.

  1. Ask the questions you're most interested in: Why did an ancestor move? Are there missing children in a gap on a family group record?
  2. For a review of questions for ancestor research, watch this video.
  3. Learn how to define research questions in this article.

C Use FindaRecord.com or Puzzilla.org to quickly identify "end-of-line" ancestors (those without recorded parents).

  1. See Project 1, Goal 9 for instructions on using Find-A-Record. Once logged in, clear all the check boxes in the colored categories on the left. Then open the Relationships category (purple) and check the Missing Father, Missing Mother, and Missing Parents boxes. A list of end-of-line ancestors is display on the right.
  2. See the Puzzilla.org Goal in Project 3 for instructions on displaying an ancestor view. Nodes without extensions are the end-of-line ancestors. To highlight these nodes so they stand out more easily, click Targets in the left pane.

D Learn about surname research and how it can be used to help you expand lines in your tree.

  1. Learn about the Geneanet site in this video.
  2. Search Geneanet.org to find your surnames of interest.
  3. Explore surname research in the right column of the Linkpendium site.

Goal 3: Form a solid strategy to research your selected lines. Vault

A good research strategy will help you get more research done in less time. It will also help you know when to spend more time on a line and when to move on to the next one.

Choices

ALearn principles of effective research.

  1. Use standard practices for genealogy research.
  2. Use the FamilySearch "Circle of Success" for doing research: Gather, Choose, Find, Evaluate, and Share.
  3. Repeat the circle for each ancestor you are researching.
  4. Read these articles for tips on getting started with your research. |
  5. Learn to focus your research efforts for better results.
  6. Read this article to learn about using the FamilySearch Wiki for research.
  7. Study the strategies for research in these articles. |

BDevise a research plan to use.

  1. Try the planning strategies shown in these documents. |
  2. Create broad goals and refine them into specific goals, as shown in this video.
  3. Understand where to look for the records you need. |
  4. Use time-saving tips from these articles to make your research more efficient. |

C Take advantage of previous research.

  1. Try the strategies in this FamilySearch Wiki document. Note: This document is titled as a United States article, but it applies well to research in any country.
  2. Learn more about using previous research in these articles. |
  3. Learn about using genealogical documents and manuscripts in your research. |
  4. Learn to examine inherited pedigrees carefully for possible problems.

D Learn how to use names and naming patterns to accelerate your research.

  1. Read this document for tips on names and naming patterns.
  2. Learn about tracing surnames in these articles from About.com. |
  3. Read this document to learn more about surname origins. There are many sub-articles, some dealing with international names.
  4. Explore surname lists, such as those found on abcgenealogy.com.
  5. Investigate single names at the Guild of One-Name Studies site.
  6. Read this About.com article to learn about using maiden names to further your research.

Goal 4: Develop your skills in searching records. Vault

The better your search skills, the more time you are likely to spend looking in the right places, and the better the results.

Choices

AImprove your search techniques.

  1. Apply best practices in the Internet searches you do, such as searching broadly at first and then more narrowly, and searching for unusual names before common names.
  2. Try the search strategies in this article when working with records in Ancestry.
  3. Use these tips when searching for ancestors with common surnames. |

BUse the Search options in the Family Tree Search screen effectively.

  1. Search historical records, search by locality, and browse collections in FamilySearch. Read these articles for ideas. |
  2. For tips on searching unindexed images read this article.
  3. Use the FamilySearch Research Wiki to search for records (FamilySearch main screen > Get Help > Research Wiki).


CUse the Search Records feature to find ancestor information on FamilySearch, Ancestry, Findmypast, and MyHeritage.

Note: You must be signed in to partner sites before accessing their records.

  1. On a Person page, click Search Records on the right.
  2. Click FamilySearch to see search results in historical records for the selected person. You can click Details or Image (if available) for a record for more information. For more information on using the Search feature, read this article or watch this video.
  3. Click Ancestry to see results from family trees or historical records for the selected person. You can click "See More Like This" for more person results, or any Matching Records link.
  4. Click Findmypast to see search results from historical records for the selected person. You can click the Note icon to see details or the Camera icon (when available) to see a record image.
  5. Click MyHeritage to see family tree results for the selected person.

DUse Google (www.google.com) to conduct effective searches for ancestor names, dates, places, etc.

  1. Learn how to use basic search variations in Google. |
  2. Learn how to use basic search variations in Google, by studying this document from Findmypast.com.
  3. Learn advanced techniques for Google searches. |

EUse variations in searches to produce better search results.

  1. Account for spelling and name variations as you search records. Read these articles for tips. |
  2. Read this article or watch this video for additional tips on using name variations.
  3. Learn to recognize possible nicknames for ancestors. |
  4. Use wildcards effectively in FamilySearch searches.
  5. Use wildcards effectively in Ancestry searches.
  6. Get the right blend of "specific" and "flexible" when doing searches with indexed records. Provide multiple search parameters to narrow the search, or use ranges to see more results.

FSave search results and images from records on your computer for later use.

  1. Do a historical records search in FamilySearch.
  2. Click the Export Results button and then click Save. Your search results will be saved in Excel spreadsheet format.
  3. Learn about using export results in this article.
  4. Save record images to your computer so you can study them in detail later.   LANG:   ES   PT

Goal 5: Solve problems and use effective research methods. Vault

When research challenges arise, they don't need to derail your progress. With some best practices and creative thinking, you can keep your family history work moving along.

Choices

A Learn to avoid common mistakes in research.

Examples of common mistakes include:

  • Collecting ancestors, without verification or sources
  • Incorrectly identifying ancestors
  • Trying to connect with a person in the past by going forward in time
  • Not recording what you find
  • Not looking for siblings of ancestors
  • Skipping generations
  1. Watch this video for more information about common mistakes in family history research.
  2. Avoid preconceived notions in research: "All the information given to me must be correct;" "I don't need to ask research questions;"All events have exact dates and places available."
  3. Learn about mistaken assumptions in genealogy research. |
  4. Understand how mistaken identities occur and how to avoid them.

BUnderstand potential problems with terms and records.

  1. Be familiar with terms that may have confusing uses in genealogy:
    • Word meanings
    • Latin terms
    • Old medical terms
    • Name and word spellings
  2. Read the articles to discover possible record problems and how to address them. |
  3. Learn about the art of estimating ("guessing") in genealogy.
  4. Learn how to do critical analysis of your research findings.

CUse effective tools and techniques.

  1. Learn how to extract and organize clues found in records.
  2. Use Inferential Genealogy techniques to work with goals, searches, records and evidences. Start with a focused goal; search broadly; understand the records; correlate the evidence; and write down the results.
  3. Use Correlation approaches to piece together records. With correlation, you can draw conclusions that are supported by the evidence, even though specific facts may be missing.
  4. Use "cluster genealogy" to research the family members of direct ancestors. This can often lead to additional clues about your direct ancestors. |
  5. Use discrepancy charts to help you evaluate facts when research is inconclusive.
  6. Learn how to abstract (summarize) the important parts of research records.

DUse creative approaches to solve problems in non-typical ways.

This can help you make progress when traditional methods aren't working well.

  1. Read this article to learn about proven tips and tactics that reach "out of the box" for results.
  2. Learn about "detective techniques" for family history in this video.
  3. Learn how to find missing ancestors by searching for their children.
  4. Use "mind maps" as explained in this article.

EUse photographs to aid in your genealogical research.

  1. Use existing photos to further your research work. Look for photos that arouse your curiosity or that seem to have a story to tell. |
  2. Learn how to date photographs in their approximate times. |
  3. Learn how to do photographic detective work.
  4. Find clues in photo captions and labels.

F Know what alternatives you can try when research is slowed or stopped.

  1. Know what questions to ask before declaring a "brick wall". |
  2. Here's a great list of 20 breakthrough items for research, from Findmypast.com.
  3. View this video or read this article for advice on cracking your genealogy brick walls.
  4. Learn tips in these articles for handling challenging situations. |

Goal 6: Use catalogs, books, and periodicals to expand your search for ancestor records. Vault

The FamilySearch Catalog and Books contain additional records that may be helpful in your searches. There are other online catalogs as well that can prove useful.

AExplore the FamilySearch Catalog.

It contains lists of resources from FamilySearch, the Family History Library in Salt Lake, FamilySearch Centers, and some partner libraries.

  1. Learn more about the FamilySearch Catalog in these articles. |
  2. In FamilySearch, click the Search menu and choose Catalog.
  3. Click one or more "Search By" fields and fill in the search information.
  4. If you have a call number for a library item or microfilm/microfiche number, click the "Search For" item and fill in the field.
  5. To limit the search to one Family History Center, select its name from the drop-down list.
  6. Click Search.
  7. Learn about using catalogs in FamilySearch and Ancestry.

BUse other online catalogs for research, such as WorldCat and Archive Grid.

       
  1. On the FamilySearch Catalog page, under "Other Catalogs to Consider", click the OCLC World Cat link. WorldCat is the world's largest network of library content and services.
  2. Watch this video to learn more about using WorldCat.
  3. On the FamilySearch Catalog page, under "Other Catalogs to Consider", click the Archive Grid link. ArchiveGrid is a collection of nearly a million archival material descriptions.

CExplore the collection of books available at FamilySearch Centers or online.

  1. In FamilySearch, click the Search menu and choose Books.
  2. Type the name of the book you are looking for in the Search field and click Search; or click Advanced Search for more choices.
  3. Learn about genealogies and books available from FamilySearch.
  4. Explore online biographies. See the FamilySearch Wiki article for resources.
  5. To visit the web site for a large public family history library, click one of the links on the Family History Books page.
  6. Learn about digital books for genealogists.

DExplore the online collection of genealogies on FamilySearch.

  1. In FamilySearch, click the Search menu and choose Genealogies.
  2. Type the name of the genealogy you are looking for in the Search field and click Search. You can look through the Ancestral File, Pedigree Resource File, International Genealogical Index (IGI), or Community Trees - descriptions for each are on the right side of the page.

EUse periodicals to find research clues.

  1. Learn how to find ancestor information in periodicals.
  2. Read this article for more information on magazines and journals.
  3. Learn about using the PERSI (Periodical Source Index) on Findmypast.com.
